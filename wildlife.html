A Deadly Hazard

Once in the environment, particularly in the oceans, plastic bags become deadly hazards for countless animal species. Their flimsy, floating nature makes them easily mistaken for food or silent traps.

  • Ingestion: Animals like sea turtles often mistake floating plastic bags for jellyfish, a common food source. Eating plastic can block their digestive systems, leading to starvation and death. Bags end up in the stomachs of many marine creatures, from whales to seabirds.
  • Entanglement: Animals can become entangled in plastic bags, leading to injury, drowning, suffocation, or an inability to escape predators.

The toll is devastating: it's estimated that plastic pollution kills over 100,000 marine animals each year.

Plastic Pollution and the Sixth Extinction

We are currently experiencing the sixth mass extinction event in Earth's history, largely driven by human activity. A major contributor identified by scientists is the rapidly increasing rate of carbon emissions, which alters climates and habitats faster than species can adapt.

Like carbon emissions, global plastic production and pollution are also increasing at an alarming rate. This relentless accumulation of non-biodegradable waste introduces toxins, disrupts ecosystems, and directly harms wildlife through ingestion and entanglement.

Therefore, while carbon emissions are a primary driver, the pervasive and persistent nature of plastic pollution, including bags, is considered another significant human pressure contributing to the ongoing sixth mass extinction.
