A Swirling Vortex of Trash

One of the most infamous results of plastic pollution in our oceans is the Great Pacific Garbage Patch (GPGP). Located in the Central North Pacific Ocean, it's not a solid island of trash, but rather a vast area where ocean currents, known as gyres, converge.

These swirling currents accumulate enormous amounts of marine debris, the vast majority of which is plastic. Bags, bottles, fishing nets, microplastics ‚Äì they all get trapped in this region.

Staggering Scale

The sheer amount of plastic concentrated in the GPGP is difficult to comprehend. Estimates suggest there are over 1.8 trillion pieces of plastic floating in this area (note: earlier estimates varied, but this is a commonly cited figure from major studies), weighing tens of thousands of tons.

The GPGP serves as a stark, large-scale example of these "gyres of floating plastic the size of a continent," demonstrating the long-lasting and far-reaching consequences of our reliance on disposable plastics.

While the GPGP is the largest and most well-known, similar accumulation zones exist in other ocean gyres around the world.
